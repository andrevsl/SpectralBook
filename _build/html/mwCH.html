3.1. 1 General Overview#

Electrical circuits are topological networks, topology in mathematical concept, which studies the space properties invariance under continous deformation. We have an arrangement of nodes, and edges, where the disposition does not matter, but the interaction. Will it matter in the microwave circuits? (Or still topological). The electromagnetic properties will change? The voltages, currents remain unchangeable if you stretch the transmission lines, what does justifies the topological idea of the electromagnetism. The symmetric operations? Graph theory also helps to visualize and decribe the interconexions, as well as, to optimize this circuits, the telecommunication and computer science have stolen the ideas.

Electric circuits are the basic representation of the electromagnetic circuits, being both the same, which the concerned metrics are Voltages and Currents. In the microwave countepart we add the beauties of the electromagnetic fields and its effects as main metrics.

3.1.1. 1.2 Justification#

In the literature we will commonly find the time analysis using narrow band signals. However wide band signals can provide a richer time-space-frequency resolution from the time motion of the current. Using Full Wave simulations of the transmission lines and waveguides, we will try to analyse practical application of the time domain analysis.

3.2. 2 Wave Voltages/Currents#

In microwave systems, where there are impedance mismatches and propagation distances are comparable to the wavelength, the concept of foward/backward waves are very important.

3.2.1. 2.1 Linear Space and Time Varying (2D)#

The voltage waves and currents for a canonical transmission line with a time dimension and one spatial dimension can be defined as

\[V(t,z)=V^{+}(t,z) e^{-\gamma_z \cdot z } + V^{-}(t,z)e^{\gamma_z \cdot z } \]
\[I(t,z)=I^{+}(t,z) e^{-\gamma_z \cdot z } + I^{-}(t,z)e^{\gamma_z \cdot z } \]

In frequency-time domain

\[V(f,z)=V^{+}(f,z) e^{-\gamma_z \cdot z } + V^{-}(f,z)e^{\gamma_z \cdot z } \]
\[I(f,z)=I^{+}(f,z) e^{-\gamma_z \cdot z } + I^{-}(f,z)e^{\gamma_z \cdot z } \]

3.2.2. 2.2 Planar Space and Time Varying (3D)#

For planar circuits, with one time dimension and two spatial dimentions

\[V(t,\overline{s})=V^{+}(t,\overline{s}) e^{-\overline{\gamma} \cdot \overline{s} } + V^{-}(t,\overline{s})e^{\overline{\gamma} \cdot \overline{s} } \]

3.3. 3 Characteristic Impedance#

For homogeneous and symmetric transmission line along their length, the geometry remains unchangeble, the characteristic impedance will be constant, i.e., space and time independent. Howerver for antennas and microwave circuits which are in general heavly assymetric, the characteristic impedance will depend on spatial coordinates.

3.3.1. 2.1 Linear Space and Time Varying (2D)#

Considering that the electrical properties of the transmission line are time-independent, only space variant

\[Z_{0}=\frac{V^{+}(z)}{I^{+}(z)} =\frac{V^{+}(z)}{I^{+}(z)} \]

3.3.2. 2.1 Planar Space and Time Varying (2D)#

or for 2D (?)

\[Z_{0x}(x,y)=?\frac{V^{+,-}_x(x,y)}{I^{+,-}_x(x,y)} =+ or - \frac{V^{-,+}_x(x,y)}{I^{-,+}_x(x,y)} \]
\[Z_{0y}(x,y)=?\frac{V^{+,-}_y(x,y)}{I^{+,-}_y(x,y)} =+ or - \frac{V^{-,+}_y(x,y)}{I^{-,+}_y(x,y)} \]
\[E^2=E_{x}^2+E_{y}^2+E_{z}^2\]
\[V^2(x,y,t)=V_{x}^2(x,y,t)+V_{y}^2(x,y,t)=[Z_{0x}(x,y,t)I_{x}(x,y,t)]^2+[Z_{0y}(x,y,t)I_{y}(x,y,t)^2\]
\[V=\int_{0}^{l} E_{x}(x,y,t) \cdot dx \approx =\sum_{0}^{n } E_{n }(n \Delta x,y) \Delta x \]

Considering the coupling between Electromagnetic Polarizations, the divergence of electric field is not zero,

\[V_x=Z_{xx}I_x +Z_{xy}I_{y}\]
\[V_y=Z_{yx}I_x +Z_{yy}I_{y}\]

Usin the Method of Moments, it is possible to obtain an approximation of the impedances

\[ Z^{xx}_0=\frac{E^{+}_{n \Delta x} \Delta x}{I^{+}_{n \Delta x}}=-\frac{E^{-}_{n \Delta x} \Delta x}{I^{-}_{n \Delta x}} \ ?\]
\[ Z^{yy}_0=\frac{E^{+}_{n \Delta y} \Delta y}{I^{+}_{n \Delta y}} \ ? \]

3.4. 4 Transmission Lines Circuit Model#

The transmission line circuit model is commonly used in electrical engineering for all application fields, specially microwave systems for communication and electronics.

  • if L and C does not vary along the distance. This equation model of the characteristic impedance of a symmetrical and homogeneous Transmission line, the characteristic impedance does not varies along the distance.

\[Z_0=\sqrt{\frac{R+j\omega L}{G+j\omega C}}\]

  • Therea plenty of methods to assess the relation between the foward/backward waves, currents, absolute waves and currents, impedances, admittances, like the impedance matrix model, the admitance matrix model, the scattering matrix model (the transfer function of wave voltages), the ABCD matrix model, all of them are used to analyze the dispersive dynamics of the microwave system. We gonna use all these methods, please refer to [orfanidis, Pozar, Balanis] For power transmssion, this quality is desired, hence the changes in characteristic impedance engenders impedance mismatches, power losses and linkages.

  • But for many applications, not restricting to the transmission of power, the characteristic impedance varies along the distance, like for filters, amplifiers, oscilators, antennas, transmission line interconexions. That’s the beauty of microwave system, the researchers and enginneers can play with the characteristic impedance and loads, to optimize the figure of merits of the envisaged application.

\begin{gather*} \Gamma_{L}=\frac{Z_{L}-Z_0}{Z_{L}+Z_0} \end{gather*}

\begin{gather*} \Gamma_{G}=\frac{Z_{G}-Z_0}{Z_{G}+Z_0} \end{gather*}

\begin{gather*} Z_{in}=Z_{0}\frac{Z_{L}+Z_0 \tanh(\gamma l)}{Z_{0}+Z_L \tanh(\gamma l)} \end{gather*}

\begin{gather*} \Z_{in}=Z_{11}-\frac{ Z_{12}Z_{21}} {Z_{22}+Z_L} \end{gather*}

\begin{gather*} \Gamma_{in}=\frac{ \Gamma_{1}+\Gamma_{L} e^{-2jkl}} {1+ \Gamma_{1}\Gamma_{L} e^{-2jkl}} \end{gather*}

\begin{gather*} \Gamma_{in}=S_{11}+\frac{ S_{12}S_{21}\Gamma_L } {1- S_{22}\Gamma_{L}} \end{gather*}

\begin{gather*} \Gamma_{in}=\frac{Z_{in}-Z_0}{Z_{in}+Z_0} \end{gather*}

In electrical circuits you can choose work with Voltages or Currents, due to its duality.
